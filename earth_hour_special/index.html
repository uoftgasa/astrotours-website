How climate change will impact your morning cup of coffee

Stephanie Gagliardi (she/her)
Climate change is expected to impact many aspects of our lives, including the production of one of the world’s favourite morning beverages, coffee. In this talk, I will give an overview of the many challenges coffee producers face as our climate changes, including shifts in growing regions and threats from devastating coffee diseases. I will focus on how farmers are managing these challenges by using and further improving sustainable growing techniques, and how us coffee drinkers can support these practices.

Stephanie is a PhD Candidate in the Department of Physical and Environmental Sciences at the University of Toronto. Her research explores the dynamic links between ecosystem processes and multi-species plant traits in biodiverse agricultural systems. Her overall research goal is to help inform sustainable farm design and management practices. Outside of work, she enjoys exploring the arts: visiting art galleries and museums, and taking drawing and ballet classes.

Why we can trust climate models

Laura Saunders (she/her)
Climate models are essential for making predictions about climate change. They allow scientists to answer the question “what would happen if…?” Unfortunately, the answer is often undesirable, which for some is a strong motivation to ignore or doubt these predictions. It is easy to convince yourself that anyone claiming to predict the future must be untrustworthy, but there are in fact many reasons that model predictions are exceptionally reliable. In this talk, I will discuss how my research contributes to the global effort to ensure that studies based on climate models are accurate.

Laura is a fourth year PhD candidate in the Department of Physics at the University of Toronto with a collaborative specialization in Environmental Studies. She completed her undergraduate degree in physics at McGill University in 2017. In her spare time, Laura enjoys climbing, skiing, walking her dog, and rewatching Gilmore Girls.

Adaptation to climate warming in a wild mustard plant

Cameron So (he/him)
Climate warming is threatening the existence of wild species and their populations worldwide. Either populations migrate, alter their behaviour or development, or rapidly adapt by evolution. In this talk, I will review how wild populations are responding to climate warming. From my research, I will also show how I exposed 7000 mustard plants to either ambient or heated conditions to evaluate the capacity for adaptation to future warming. This talk will dive into observations of the natural world, as well as evolutionary principles that are now recently being considered by conservation practitioners.

Cameron So is a PhD student in the Department of Biology at McGill University. He previously completed his M.Sc (2020) in the Department of Ecology and Evolutionary Biology at the University of Toronto, studying the capacity for adaptation to climate warming in an annual field mustard. His current research focuses on conserving plants at- risk in Canada. Outside of school, Cameron enjoys hiking, frisbee, and attempting to grow heirloom tomatoes
